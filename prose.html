Romance: a literary dream narrative

P. Mich. inv. 3378 Greek - Unknown provenance, Egypt - Late 2nd c. C.E.

An unknown literary text describing a dream-narrative, including an interaction with a ghost and death imagery. Due to the mention of the name Seueris, it has been suggested that the story took place, at least in part, in Egypt.

The Wonders Beyond Thule

P.Mich.inv. 5. Greek – Unknown provenance, Egypt. –2nd-3rd c. C.E.

This fragment is likely to be from Antonius Diogenes’ lost novel The Wonders Beyond Thule. The text describes a magician, probably Paapis, performing a spell. Thus, this fragment might allude to the fate of Derkyllis and her lover Mantineas, who were bewitched by Paapis and condemned to live by night and sleep by day.

For more information, please see the entry on APIS.

Parthenope and Metiochos

P.Mich.inv. 3402. Greek. – Unknown provenance. –3rd c. C.E.

This is one of only a small handful of papyrus fragments that remain of the ancient Greek novel of Parthenope and Metiochos. Although the complete novel no longer exists in ancient Greek, enough remains to establish this fragment as the origin of the Persian story of Vāmiq and ‘Azrā.’ The appearance in other fragments of the names “Polykrates,” “Anaximenes,” and “Hegesipyle” suggests that the story was based on the previously unnamed daughter (here called “Parthenope,” a name which implies virginity) of sixth-century B.C.E. tyrant Polykrates of Samos.

For more information, please see the entry on APIS.

Pamphilos and Eurydice

P.Mich.inv. 3793. Greek – Oxyrhynchohos, Egypt. –1st c. C.E.

This fragment depicts a love affair set in Cyprus between two figures by the names of Phamphilos and Eurydice. Pamphilos discovers the bloodied clothing of Eurydice by a spring and mistakenly assumes that she has been devoured by beasts. This is an evident parallel to Pyramus and Thisbe from Ovid’s Metamorphoses. However, it is unknown whether this tragedy ends in the same way.

For more information, please see the entry on APIS.
